IBM Research  ·  Summer 1958

The Problem
McCarthy Noticed

He chose a sample problem: symbolically differentiate algebraic expressions. The derivative of x² is 2x. LISP could represent these as list structures and apply the rules recursively. He wrote the definition. It was beautiful. Clean. Recursive. Mathematically elegant. And then he noticed something.

—
—
Expression tree — diff(x²)
OBSERVATION: After each call, the allocator holds
no record of which nodes are still reachable.
There is no mechanism to reclaim the intermediates.
THEY SIMPLY REMAIN.
Heap — all allocations
Memory blocks 0 nodes
"Every time the differentiation function recursed, it created new list structures to hold intermediate expressions — churning into existence and then becoming irrelevant as the recursion unwound and the final answer assembled. Nobody freed them. There was no mechanism to free them. They simply accumulated, silently, for the duration of the program."
— McCarthy's 1958 discovery  ·  The origin of garbage collection
